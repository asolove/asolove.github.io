Constraint programming talk, JSConf 2013

I am very interested in how computers can extend rather than replace human thinking. Most user interfaces today rely on humans to provide input but then have computers make the actual decisions. Constraint programming is one model in which human decisions and computer logic can coexist to extend the limits of human thinking. In this talk, I explained the basics of constraint programming, show several basic examples, and speculate on the future of constraint interfaces.

Smallest federated wiki 2011

Ward Cunningham, the inventor of the wiki, is now exploring a major change to the idea, using a decentralized editing model similar to git. His project, Smallest federated wiki, had a solid API and a great interface concept, but some of the details of getting it to work in the browser had proved difficult to implement. I contributed my browser knowledge, starting with this browser history, animations, and scrolling work. My work made the interface more fluid and intuitive.

Founder, Snowdrop 2015

Currently in a private beta, Snowdrop is a mobile webapp that helps families to fight post-partum depression. My family has struggled with post-partum depression and its aftermath since the birth of our first child. Through the recovery, we learned that while medication and therapy play an important role, we also had to develop new habits of thought, reflection, and communication. We've tried to turn those habits into a repeatable process and are currently experimenting with it. The software is built on Rails, Heroku and Twilio.

Webmaster, The Millennial Experience Mobile Exhibit 2015

View the archived site or some of the resulting responses.

I built a mobile website to accompany an exhibit on millennials' existential questions that was displayed at Europe's largest summer festival, the Meeting at Rimini. With such an enormous audience, mostly using old Android phones on an over-crowded cell network, the site needed to be very fast to load. But it also needed to be fast to build and update in several languages, because the content was only available shortly before the exhibition began, and then changed rapidly.

I used React as the site's foundation, providing me the convenience of working in JavaScript and its ecosystem of libraries, while still allowing the finished site to be burned out as static HTML with no client-side code.

Pages loaded in less than a second even on slow cell connections, but content could be updated across the site and deployed in seconds.

Deco 2010

For several years I worked as a freelancer doing typesetting, web design, and development work for various clients. During that time I developed a strong distrust for my clients' descriptions of what they wanted. It was only when we got to visual representations that their opinions began to be consistent. Deco was an experimental online moodboarding tool where clients and I could work together to look for inspiration and agree on the meanings of the words they used.

Built in 2010, Deco was a very early experiment in using Node.js, websockets, the html5 File API, and multitouch events.
